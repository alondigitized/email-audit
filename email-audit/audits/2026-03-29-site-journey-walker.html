# Walker Miles — Skechers.com Journey Review

Date: March 29, 2026 | Yesterday's Score: 4/10

Executive Summary

I'm Walker Miles, 62 years old. I walked through Skechers.com on my phone today looking for a pair of Hands Free Slip-ins. The good news: the site recognized me, showed my rewards points every step of the way, and the Men's navigation was clean. The bad news: after all that browsing, I hit "Add to Cart" and ended up with an empty cart. Zero items. Zero dollars. After spending fifteen minutes finding the right shoe, that's a gut punch. Search also showed me Women's shoes first. That's the kind of stuff that makes a guy close the tab and drive to the store instead.

Business Impact Score: 5/10

Up 1 point from yesterday (4/10).

The personalization experience improved noticeably — my name and rewards points were front and center throughout the journey. But the cart failure is a conversion-killer. If I can't actually buy the shoe, nothing else matters. Search relevance remains a problem for a male shopper.

What's Working

Personalization is genuinely good now.

Every page after login showed: *"Walker, You have enough points for a $5 reward."* That's my name. That's my balance. That feels real. It makes me feel like a loyal customer, not just a visitor.

BOGO deal is impossible to miss.

"BOGO 50% OFF Applied at Cart" was in the top banner on nearly every page. As someone who's price-conscious, I notice that. It would've motivated me to buy two pairs if the cart had actually worked.

Men's navigation is clean and direct.

Step 4 showed me a tidy list: Shoes, Comfort Technologies, Hands Free Slip-ins right there as a quick link. No hunting. I could read it without squinting. The touch targets felt big enough. This was the best screen of the whole journey.

The Hands Free Slip-ins category page is well-organized.

258 results, clear heading, description of the technology, and filter buttons for Wide Fit and Extra Wide Fit right at the top. That's exactly what I need — I want wide fit options without digging.

Product page communicated value clearly.

$135.00 price, "Buy 1 Get 1 50% off!" prominently shown, star rating, and the Slip-ins badge. Good information density without feeling cluttered on a phone screen.

What's Weak

The cart is broken. This is critical.

I went through the whole flow — found my shoe, scrolled through sizes, hit Add to Cart — and arrived at an empty cart. "There are no items in your cart." $0.00 total. The Add to Cart screen showed a "Please select a size" warning, which suggests the size selection step didn't register or wasn't completed before redirect. Whatever the cause, I ended up with nothing. That's a lost sale. Full stop.

Homepage hero features a woman, not men's content.

Step 1 shows "Spring Shopping Guide" with a woman in a field. I'm a 62-year-old man shopping for men's slip-ins. Nothing on that hero spoke to me. No men's imagery, no comfort tech angle, no Slip-ins call-out. It felt like I landed on the wrong store.

Steps 1, 2, and 3 looked identical.

The homepage, popup dismissal, and login screens all appeared the same — the same Spring Shopping Guide image. If I logged in, I couldn't tell. There was no confirmation, no "Welcome back, Walker," no visible change. The personalized rewards banner was there, but the login moment itself was invisible. Good that cookies may have handled it seamlessly, but confusing that nothing changed visually.

Search returned Women's shoes first.

Searching "slip in" pulled 1,017 results but the first two visible products were both labeled Women's. I'm a logged-in male Skechers Plus member — the site knows who I am. Showing me women's shoes first is a relevance failure. That's frustrating on a small phone screen where I can only see 2 products at a time.

Size selection flow could cause errors.

The size grid on Step 7 required me to pick a size before adding to cart — fine — but the "Please select a size" error appeared after I attempted to add. Better UX would be to prevent the button tap until a size is chosen, rather than letting me tap and fail.

Recommendations

1. Fix the cart add failure immediately. This is the #1 priority. A broken Add to Cart means zero revenue from this entire journey. Investigate whether the size selection state is persisting correctly before the cart request fires.

2. Personalize the homepage hero for logged-in male users. I logged in and saw a women's lifestyle image. Show me men's comfort shoes, Slip-ins, or a male-focused seasonal story. You already know I'm Walker and that I'm a Skechers Plus member.

3. Filter search results by gender when user is logged in. I'm a known male shopper. "Slip in" should return Men's results first — or at minimum give me a one-tap Men's filter at the top.

4. Add a visible login confirmation moment. When I log in (or when cookies restore my session), show me something — even just a brief "Welcome back, Walker!" before loading the page. It builds trust and confirms the session worked.

5. Disable "Add to Cart" button until size is selected. Grey it out. Don't let me tap it only to fail. That error screen feels like a mistake I made, even when it's the site's fault.

Bottom Line

Skechers knows who I am — I could see that throughout the whole journey. My name, my points, my BOGO deal. That part is better than yesterday. But I still walked away with an empty cart and a frustrated feeling. The personalization raised my expectations and then the broken cart crashed them. Fix the cart. Fix search. Put some men's content on that homepage for a logged-in male member. Those three changes would turn this from a 5 into an 8 fast.

## Technical Audit

## Technical Summary

Site runs on Salesforce Commerce Cloud (Demandware). TTFB is excellent across most pages (31–38ms) with one significant outlier on View Cart (583ms). A third-party CSS dependency (Stylitics) is broken due to CORS misconfiguration. An SFMC cart-tracking API call is failing with a 422 due to a data type mismatch. Accessibility has 33 axe violations including 4 critical. LCP and CLS data were unavailable for this audit.

## Accessibility

4 Critical violations:

  • aria-required-parent (Homepage): ARIA role elements not wrapped in required parent roles — screen readers will misinterpret structure.
  • aria-required-children (View Product): Parent ARIA roles missing required child roles.
  • aria-valid-attr-value (View Product): One or more ARIA attributes have invalid values (e.g., aria-*="" or referencing non-existent IDs).
  • button-name (View Product): Buttons with no accessible label — likely icon-only buttons missing aria-label or aria-labelledby.

4 Serious violations:

  • link-name (Homepage): Anchor elements with no discernible text — likely image links without alt or aria-label.
  • aria-dialog-name (View Product): Modal/dialog elements missing accessible names; keyboard and screen reader users cannot identify dialog purpose.
  • aria-input-field-name (View Product): ARIA input roles without labels.
  • link-in-text-block (Men's Shoes): In-text links differentiated only by color, failing WCAG 1.4.1 (non-color distinction required).
  • color-contrast (View Product): Foreground/background contrast below WCAG AA 4.5:1 threshold.

Landmark/structure issues (multiple pages):

  • Duplicate <main> landmark (Homepage).
  • <aside>/complementary not at top level (Homepage, Men's Shoes).
  • Non-unique landmark roles (Homepage, Men's Shoes).
  • Content outside landmark regions (Homepage, Men's Shoes).
  • Incorrect heading order (Homepage).

13 additional violations not enumerated in input data.

View Cart TTFB spike (583ms): 15–19× slower than all other pages. Likely caused by server-side cart session resolution, inventory/pricing lookups, or personalization rendering (Monetate, iGoDigital) blocking the initial response. This is a backend issue, not a network issue.

Third-party script bloat (Homepage/Login): 15+ async third-party scripts loaded on every page — TikTok Pixel (2 scripts), Pinterest, Reddit Ads, Bing UET, Yahoo YTC (loaded twice — duplicate), Google Tag Manager, Google Ads, GA4, Monetate (2 scripts), Quantum Metric, Bloomreach, Impact, Tune, WebFontLoader. Each is an additional DNS lookup + TCP handshake. While async, they contend for main-thread bandwidth and inflate TBT/TTI.

Duplicate Yahoo pixel: vice-managed-src="https://s.yimg.com/wi/ytc.js" appears twice in <head> — double-firing analytics events.

LCP/CLS unavailable: Cannot assess largest contentful paint or layout shift. These are the primary Core Web Vitals ranking factors.

## Mobile Optimization

  • --vh: 6.640000000000001px CSS custom property set inline on <html> via JavaScript — standard mobile 100vh workaround for address bar height changes. The floating-point precision (6.640000000000001) suggests no rounding is applied; harmless but slightly wasteful.
  • TrustArc consent management (vice-managed-src attributes) defers third-party scripts until consent is given — correct implementation for CCPA/GDPR.
  • WebFontLoader (v1.6.28, loaded from cdnjs) loads Mulish in 5 weights (n3–n7). This is a render-blocking concern until fonts resolve; the wf-active class on <html> confirms fonts loaded successfully for this session.

## Console & Network Errors

Stylitics CORS failure (2 errors):

`

Access to CSS stylesheet at 'https://web-assets.stylitics.com/style.css?base=hotspots&version=1f1e003a2c'

has been blocked by CORS policy: No 'Access-Control-Allow-Origin' header

`

The Stylitics outfit/hotspot widget CSS is being requested both as a <link> stylesheet and via XHR — both blocked. The Stylitics CDN is not returning Access-Control-Allow-Origin: https://www.skechers.com. Result: the Stylitics widget renders unstyled or not at all. Fix: Stylitics must add the appropriate CORS header on their CDN; Skechers should escalate to their Stylitics account team.

SFMC iGoDigital cart tracking 422:

`

422 https://nova.collect.igodigital.com/c2/100007688/track_cart

payload: {"url":"...","user_info":{"email":"8107766165","details":...}}

`

The email field in the cart tracking payload contains "8107766165" — a phone number, not an email address. The iGoDigital API rejects it with 422 Unprocessable Entity because the value fails email format validation. Root cause: the identity resolution layer is populating email with the user's phone number. This breaks Salesforce Marketing Cloud's cart abandonment tracking. Fix: audit the GTM/SFCC data layer variable mapped to user_info.email on cart pages; ensure it sources from the email field, not phone.

Two additional ERR_FAILED entries correspond to the Stylitics failures above.

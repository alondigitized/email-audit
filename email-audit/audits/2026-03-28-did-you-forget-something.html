Skechers Experience Review

Did you forget something?

FromSKECHERS <hello@msgs.skechers.com>
Received2026-03-28 23:01 UTC
Score6/10

1. Executive Summary

A classic abandoned-cart recovery email from Skechers that follows the textbook playbook: show the abandoned product, provide a direct CTA to complete the order, then cross-sell with recommended alternatives and a BOGO incentive. The visual hierarchy is clear — hero headline, product image, orange "Complete Your Order" button — but the email overshoots by stacking too many secondary modules beneath the cart recovery core. The recommended products, BOGO 50% Off banner, category navigation, texting opt-in, app download, and curbside pickup sections turn what should be a focused cart nudge into a miniature homepage. The core job of this email is singular: get the shopper back to their cart. Everything after the first CTA dilutes that mission.

2. Business Impact Score

6 / 10

Cart abandonment emails are the highest-ROI automated flow in e-commerce. This one will recover some carts from the strength of its timing and product reminder alone, but the cluttered layout and competing CTAs reduce the urgency that drives immediate action. The BOGO offer, while valuable, belongs in the subject line or hero — not buried below the fold.

3. What's Working

  • "WE NOTICED YOU NOTICING US..." headline is attention-grabbing and playful. It re-engages the shopper without being pushy or guilt-tripping ("You left something behind" is overdone). Good brand voice.
  • Abandoned product displayed prominently — the Max Cushioning Knit 7.0 is shown with a clear image and product name, which is the most important element in any cart recovery email.
  • "COMPLETE YOUR ORDER" CTA is high-contrast orange, above the fold, and uses action-oriented language. This is the primary conversion driver and it's correctly positioned.
  • "HERE'S SOME MORE GREAT OPTIONS WE THINK YOU'LL LOVE" section is a reasonable cross-sell inclusion — if the shopper abandoned because of indecision, showing alternatives could re-engage them.
  • Buy One, Get One 50% Off banner is a strong incentive that could tip a hesitant buyer. The creative is clean and the offer is immediately legible.
  • Footer utility stack (texting opt-in, app download, curbside pickup, store locator) is comprehensive and well-organized.

4. What's Weak

  • Too many modules below the fold. After "Complete Your Order," the email adds recommended products, "Shop All Best Sellers," BOGO 50% Off, category navigation (Women, Men, Kids, Clothing, New Arrivals, Sale), texting opt-in, app download, and curbside pickup. That is seven secondary modules fighting for attention after the primary CTA. Each one reduces the probability of the shopper clicking the one button that matters.
  • The BOGO 50% Off offer is buried mid-scroll. If Skechers is running a sitewide BOGO, this is a conversion accelerator that should appear in the subject line or directly beneath the abandoned product — not after a grid of recommended shoes the shopper never asked to see.
  • No price shown on the abandoned product. The Max Cushioning Knit 7.0 is displayed without a price. Cart recovery emails are most effective when they show exactly what the shopper will pay. The absence forces the shopper to click through just to remember the price, adding friction.
  • No urgency mechanism. There is no mention of limited stock, cart expiration, or time-limited pricing. The email reads as a polite reminder rather than a reason to act now.
  • Subject line is generic. "Did you forget something?" is one of the most overused cart abandonment subject lines in e-commerce. It doesn't differentiate Skechers from any other brand's cart recovery email.
  • Category navigation buttons (Women, Men, Kids, Clothing, New Arrivals, Sale) are filler. These replicate the website's top nav and add zero value in a cart abandonment context. The shopper already browsed — they don't need to be sent back to the category page.
  • Recommended product images are small. The cross-sell shoes are rendered at a size where style details are hard to distinguish, reducing their ability to spark interest.

5. Recommendations

1. Cut everything below the BOGO offer. The email should end after: (1) abandoned product, (2) Complete Your Order CTA, (3) recommended alternatives, (4) BOGO incentive. Remove the category nav, texting opt-in, app download, and curbside sections — these belong in welcome or post-purchase flows, not cart recovery.

2. Move the BOGO offer up. Place "Buy One, Get One 50% Off" directly beneath the abandoned product as a conversion accelerator: "Complete your order and get your second pair at 50% off."

3. Show the price. Display the product price (and the BOGO savings) next to the Max Cushioning Knit 7.0 image.

4. Add urgency. Include a low-stock indicator, a cart reservation timer, or a time-limited incentive ("Your cart is saved for 48 hours").

5. Rewrite the subject line. Test something product-specific: "Your Max Cushioning Knit 7.0 is waiting + BOGO 50% Off" — or urgency-driven: "Still thinking it over? Your cart won't last forever."

6. Increase cross-sell image size. Show 2 larger product images instead of 3–4 small thumbnails. Make each product visually compelling enough to click.

6. Bottom Line

This email has the right bones — abandoned product, strong CTA, relevant cross-sells, and a BOGO incentive — but it's buried under unnecessary secondary content that turns a focused recovery email into a brand catalog. Cart abandonment is a sniper shot, not a shotgun blast. Strip it down to the essentials, lead with urgency and the BOGO offer, show the price, and this becomes a high-performing recovery email instead of an average one.

This is a cart abandonment email sent via SendGrid (geopod-ismtpd infrastructure). The email uses a mix of HTTPS and potentially HTTP image assets. No QA report was available for automated checks, so this assessment is based on manual HTML inspection and the rendered screenshot. 2. Link & Tracking Issues

SendGrid click tracking — All links route through SendGrid's click-tracking infrastructure. UTM parameters and destination URLs are encoded in the redirect payload. Without decrypting the redirect URLs, UTM correctness cannot be verified from the HTML source alone.

Abandoned product link — The "COMPLETE YOUR ORDER" CTA presumably links back to the cart or product page. If the link resolves to a generic page rather than a pre-populated cart, the recovery flow breaks. This must be verified via a live click test.

Multiple competing CTAs — The email contains at minimum 10+ clickable elements: Complete Your Order, each recommended product, Shop All Best Sellers, BOGO banner, Women, Men, Kids, Clothing, New Arrivals, Sale, texting opt-in, app download, curbside pickup, and social icons. This dilutes click attribution and makes it harder to measure which module drives recovery.

3. Rendering & Accessibility

Screenshot review shows clean rendering. No broken images, overlapping elements, or layout shifts visible in the webview render. The email appears to be well-constructed from a rendering standpoint.

Category navigation buttons are rendered as full-width text blocks with minimal padding — functional but visually heavy, contributing to the email's excessive length.

Mobile rendering — The email appears to be mobile-responsive based on the screenshot (single-column layout). The category buttons stack vertically, which is correct but adds significant scroll depth on mobile.

Alt text status unknown — Without the QA report, the alt text coverage on product images cannot be confirmed. The abandoned product image and cross-sell images should have descriptive alt text for accessibility and broken-image fallback.

4. Personalization & Merge Tokens

Abandoned product is personalized — The Max Cushioning Knit 7.0 in Bisque appears to be dynamically inserted based on cart contents. This is the expected behavior for a cart abandonment flow.

No visible merge token issues — No raw merge tokens (e.g., %%FIRST_NAME%%, {{product.name}}) visible in the rendered output. The personalization engine appears to have resolved correctly.

No name personalization — The email does not address the recipient by name. Adding "Hi [First Name]" would increase open-to-click rates by establishing familiarity.

5. Compliance

Sender domain: msgs.skechers.com — a subdomain of skechers.com, consistent with transactional/automated sends being separated from marketing sends.

CAN-SPAM compliance — The footer area is visible in the screenshot with standard utility links. Physical address and unsubscribe mechanism should be present in the full footer (truncated in the screenshot). These must be confirmed in the full HTML source.

SPF/DKIM: SendGrid handles authentication for msgs.skechers.com. Alignment depends on whether DKIM d= matches the subdomain. Without raw headers, authentication status cannot be confirmed.

6. Email-to-Site Continuity

Cart recovery link is the critical continuity point. The "COMPLETE YOUR ORDER" CTA must resolve to a pre-populated cart page — not a generic homepage or product listing. If the cart session has expired server-side, the link should at minimum resolve to the product page for the Max Cushioning Knit 7.0 with a clear "Add to Cart" option.

BOGO 50% Off — If this offer is mentioned in the email, the landing page should reflect the same offer without requiring a promo code. Any disconnect between the email promise and the site experience will increase bounce rate.

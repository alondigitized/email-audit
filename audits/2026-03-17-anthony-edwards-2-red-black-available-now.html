Executive Summary

This is not a Skechers email. It is a competitor send from adidas announcing the Anthony Edwards 2 basketball shoe in a new Red & Black colorway. Reviewed here as competitive intelligence.

The email is a clean, single-purpose product drop announcement. Adidas uses Anthony Edwards' NBA profile effectively as the lead visual asset and keeps the hierarchy simple: athlete hero → product shot → SHOP NOW. The structure is competent but not exceptional. The email leans entirely on the celebrity name and colorway novelty — there is no urgency driver, no loyalty hook, and no cross-sell depth. A few technical issues (HTTP images, empty plain-text fallback, a visible dynamic content placeholder) indicate this went out with QA gaps that a brand of this scale shouldn't be shipping.

Business Impact Score

6 / 10

Solid execution of a basic product drop. The athlete branding does the heavy lifting. Structural simplicity keeps the path to purchase clear, but the lack of scarcity signaling, loyalty integration, or secondary product discovery leaves revenue on the table. Technical issues are a minor but real embarrassment at this send volume.

What's Working

  • Athlete-forward hero. Anthony Edwards portrait leads the email. Name recognition is the primary conversion driver and the layout correctly makes him the first thing you see.
  • Clear subject line. "Anthony Edwards 2: Red & Black available now" is specific, searchable, and accurately sets expectations. No bait-and-switch.
  • Focused CTA hierarchy. "SHOP NOW" is the dominant action. No competing offers dilute the path to purchase.
  • Multiple product entry points. The email appears to present the AE2 in more than one configuration (separate link destinations), giving the subscriber multiple shots at a click.
  • Store module. "FIND STORE NOW" is a smart inclusion for a performance basketball shoe — in-store try-on is still relevant in this category.

What's Weak

  • No urgency or scarcity. "Available now" is a statement, not a reason to act today. No limited colorway framing, no "while supplies last," no time-bound element.
  • No loyalty or membership touchpoint. An adidas adiClub mention (early access, points, member exclusives) is absent. For a named athlete shoe at likely premium price, leaving the membership hook out is a missed conversion and retention opportunity.
  • Shallow copy. The hero headline is "The latest Anthony Edwards 2 basketball shoe is here." That's a description, not a story. No performance claim, no colorway backstory, no connection to Edwards' season or brand narrative.
  • Store module creates split intent. Placing a "FIND STORE NOW" module in a purchase-intent email without context (e.g., "Available in-store and online") forces the reader to make a channel decision without guidance. It can leak clicks from the ecomm path.
  • Dynamic content placeholder exposed. The link text "Display images to show real-time content" is a rendering fallback that should never be visible to recipients. Someone got lazy with QA.
  • Empty plain-text fallback. Zero characters. Subscribers on plain-text clients see nothing. This is a deliverability risk and an accessibility failure.

Recommendations

1. Add scarcity language. Even "Limited colorway" or "Get it before it sells out" in the hero copy would lift urgency materially. This is table stakes for a drop-model product.

2. Wire in the loyalty program. A single line — "adiClub members earn points on this drop" — activates a conversion lever and reinforces program value. Adidas is leaving that entirely on the shelf here.

3. Strengthen the hero copy. Tie the Red & Black colorway to something: Edwards' team colors, a specific game moment, a design detail. "Available now" copy at this price point undersells the product.

4. Fix the HTTP image assets. Six arrow images are loading over HTTP. Modern email clients and spam filters will block these. Migrate to HTTPS — no exceptions.

5. Build a plain-text fallback. Zero chars is not a fallback, it's a gap. Even a minimal text version with the key headline and a short URL improves deliverability and accessibility.

6. Resolve the dynamic content placeholder. The visible "Display images to show real-time content" link text indicates a broken real-time content module. Either fix the module integration or remove it — never ship this to a live list.

7. Clarify the omnichannel message. If the shoe is available both online and in-store, say so explicitly in the hero section. The store module mid-email feels like an afterthought rather than a coordinated omnichannel offer.

Bottom Line

A competent but punching-below-weight product launch email. Adidas has a premium asset in Anthony Edwards and a clearly desirable product, but the email does the minimum to introduce it. No urgency, no loyalty integration, no narrative. Clean template execution gets them a passing grade; the technical issues (HTTP images, empty plain-text, exposed placeholder) are unforced errors for a brand sending at this scale. Skechers should study the clean visual hierarchy and athlete-first framing — and use this as a reminder that even top-tier brands ship sloppy.

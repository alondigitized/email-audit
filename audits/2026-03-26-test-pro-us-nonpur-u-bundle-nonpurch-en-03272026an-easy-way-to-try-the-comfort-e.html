Executive Summary

A straightforward non-purchaser acquisition email anchored by a BOGO 50% off Spring Sale. The offer is clear and the layout is functional, but the email relies almost entirely on category browsing to convert — there's no product storytelling, no social proof, and no personalization that would justify sending a separate "bundle" variant. The biggest red flag is the subject line leaking internal test nomenclature directly into the inbox.

Business Impact Score: 5/10

The offer is solid; the execution is generic. It will generate some clicks but leaves significant conversion potential on the table.

What's Working

  • Offer clarity. "Buy One, Get One 50% Off" reads instantly at the hero — no ambiguity about the mechanic.
  • Dual CTAs in hero. "Shop Now" and "Find a Store" serve both online and in-store buyers without competing.
  • Category coverage. Women's, Men's, and Kids' are each given their own visual row with a dedicated CTA, making navigation efficient.
  • App + Curbside Pickup utility strip. Practical bottom modules address different fulfillment preferences without being intrusive.

What's Weak

  • Subject line is a QA failure. [Test]: PRO_US_NONPUR_U_BUNDLE_NONPURCH_EN_03272026... is fully visible to recipients. This is a live send with a test subject — a significant trust and brand credibility hit.
  • Preview text is broken. The preview renders raw URLs and placeholder tokens rather than readable copy. Recipients see Skechers® https://view.emails... https://www.skechers.com?src=... — this destroys first-impression metrics before the email is even opened.
  • No hero product imagery. The hero is a text/color block with no shoe photography. For a footwear brand, this is a missed opportunity to create desire at the fold.
  • Category rows are identical in structure. Women's, Men's, and Kids' each show 4 product thumbnails + a button — there's no visual hierarchy or editorial point of view. Nothing signals which products are trending or recommended.
  • No personalization or hook for non-purchasers. This is explicitly a non-purchaser segment, yet nothing in the email addresses hesitation, introduces the brand, or offers a reason-to-believe beyond the discount. The copy doesn't earn the click.
  • "Discount applied at cart" fine print is easy to miss. Critical offer condition buried below the CTA buttons in small type.

Recommendations

1. Do not send with this subject line. Fix the test prefix and internal code string before any live deployment.

2. Rewrite the preview text to lead with the offer: "Buy one, get one 50% off — Spring Sale is on now."

3. Add a hero shoe image. Lead with one aspirational product shot. Non-purchasers need visual desire, not just a deal.

4. Add one non-purchaser-specific hook — a brief intro line ("First time? Here's why everyone loves Skechers comfort") anchors the discount in brand value rather than pure price promotion.

5. Differentiate at least one category row with a "bestseller" or "trending now" badge to create urgency and editorial curation.

6. Surface the offer condition ("discount applied at cart") more visibly — either as a callout badge on the hero or as bolded copy directly under the BOGO headline.

Bottom Line

The promotional mechanics are sound, but this email reads like a template with swapped-in copy rather than a crafted non-purchaser journey. The subject line and preview text issues alone would tank open rates and damage sender reputation — those must be fixed before this goes live. Assuming those are resolved, the email needs a product image in the hero and a single non-purchaser trust signal to move from "functional" to "effective."

## Technical Audit — Skechers BUNDLE_NONPURCH_EN_03272026

1. Technical Summary

This test email carries a systemic HTTP image-serving issue affecting every hosted asset on image.emails.skechers.com, which will cause broken images across Gmail, Outlook, and any client that enforces HTTPS for remote content. A second-priority concern is the un-stripped test subject prefix that must be removed before live deployment.

2. Link & Tracking Issues

HTTP tracking pixel — mixed content

  • http://www.ink1000.com/p/up/7076d8b15a776085/o.gif?mi_u=8103142203&mi_ecmp=PRO_US_NONPUR_U_BUNDLE_NONPURCH_EN_03272026
  • Served over plain HTTP. Gmail proxies HTTP resources; Outlook 365 blocks them silently. Switch to HTTPS or verify ink1000 supports it.

26 click-redirect links skipped by QA probe

  • All CTA links route through click.emails.skechers.com — destination URLs and UTM parameters could not be verified. No broken destinations confirmed, but continuity cannot be asserted (see §6).

Krux/DMP user-match pixels

  • beacon.krxd.net/1x1_usermatch.gif (×2) and beacon.krxd.net/1x1_ad_impression.gif — served over HTTPS, no issue. Confirm these are permitted under current privacy disclosures.

Return Path seed pixel

  • pixel.app.returnpath.net/pixel.gif — served over HTTP (no https:// scheme). Same mixed-content risk as ink1000.

Plain-text URL density

  • QA flagged 4,149 of 5,792 plain-text characters are URLs (71.6%). This exceeds the ~60% threshold used by several spam filters. Plain-text version needs prose content, not just raw redirect URLs.

Tracking/spacer pixels should carry alt="" (already done on the SFMC open pixel). Decorative or content images with missing alt text fail WCAG 2.1 AA §1.1.1 and render as broken-image icons when images are blocked.

Zoom prevention

  • <meta name="viewport" content="width=device-width, initial-scale=1, maximum-scale=1" /> — maximum-scale=1 prevents pinch-to-zoom on iOS. Removes accessibility for low-vision users. Remove maximum-scale=1.

Global text-size adjustment disabled

  • * { -webkit-text-size-adjust: none; } applied to all elements. This suppresses iOS's automatic text legibility scaling across the entire email. Scope this rule only to elements where it is intentionally needed, or remove it.

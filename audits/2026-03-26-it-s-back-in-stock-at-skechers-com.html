It's Back in Stock at SKECHERS.com

FromSKECHERS <no-reply@emails.skechers.com>

Score5/10

Executive Summary

A transactional back-in-stock alert that does its core job adequately but leaves significant conversion opportunity on the table. The product is identified, the CTA is present, and there's a cross-sell module — but the hero is undersized, the product image is tiny, and the emotional urgency that makes back-in-stock emails effective is almost entirely absent.

Business Impact Score: 5/10

Functional but underperforming. The email fulfills the basic promise (item is available again) without creating any real sense of urgency or desire.

What's Working

  • Clear subject-line alignment — the email opens with "BACK IN STOCK ALERT!" in bold, which matches the subject line immediately.
  • Product details are explicit — Style #, Color, Size, and Price are all visible in a structured list, confirming this is the exact item the recipient waited for.
  • Single, unambiguous CTA — "BUY IT NOW" button is prominent and directly below the product block.
  • Cross-sell module — "You Might Also Be Interested In..." shows four product thumbnails with names and presumably prices, which is smart placement for recipients who find the item already gone again.

What's Weak

  • Hero image is postage-stamp sized — the product photo is tiny relative to the email canvas. For a back-in-stock alert, the product image should be the emotional centerpiece; here it barely registers.
  • No urgency language — the subject and header say "back in stock" but nothing in the body communicates scarcity, time pressure, or demand (e.g., "Limited quantity," "Selling fast," "Only a few left"). This is the single most important driver of conversion in this email type and it's missing.
  • "Remember that style you love? It's back, but not for long. Shop it today before it's gone" header copy is buried in small gray text immediately below the logo — it reads like a disclaimer rather than a hook.
  • No product lifestyle or contextual imagery — the product thumbnail is catalog/product-page style and very small; a larger, worn-in-context image would drive purchase intent harder.
  • Customer service number callout feels like dead weight at this point in the conversion flow.

Recommendations

1. Triple the product image size — make it the visual anchor of the email, not an afterthought in a spec table.

2. Add scarcity messaging — a line like "Only a few pairs left at your size" directly above or below the CTA would sharply lift urgency.

3. Elevate the preheader copy — move "It's back, but not for long. Shop it today before it's gone" into larger, styled headline text rather than small gray body copy.

4. Add a second CTA after the cross-sell module — recipients who browse the recommendations should have an easy re-entry path to the original item.

5. Remove or condense the customer service block — not relevant to the conversion moment; it diffuses focus.

Bottom Line

This email communicates the facts but misses the feeling. Back-in-stock emails work because they create a fleeting window — the item is available *now*, but maybe not for long. That tension needs to be felt viscerally in the design. Larger product image, scarcity copy, and one line of urgency would likely meaningfully improve conversion without any structural rebuild.

The email is structurally sound with standard Outlook/mobile compatibility boilerplate, but has three confirmed issues: a missing plain-text fallback, several images served over HTTP instead of HTTPS, and missing alt text on multiple images. SPF/DKIM authentication could not be verified through the relay.

2. Link & Tracking Issues

  • 26 tracking links route through click.emails.skechers.com (Salesforce Marketing Cloud click-redirect). Links were not probed — destination URLs and UTM parameters could not be verified.
  • Open-pixel beacon fires via click.emails.skechers.com/open.aspx?NBJUC24YDGGENDIIFTWBSORU5A... — standard SFMC implementation, no anomaly.
  • Three additional third-party beacon pixels fire on load via beacon.krxd.net for Salesforce DMP user-matching and ad impression tracking. These are zero-dimension (width="0" height="0") with display:none, consistent with standard practice.
